Ouagadougou

History

Ouagadougou, commonly known as Ouaga, is the capital and the big city of Burkina Faso, with a population of 2.5 million in 2015. It is the cultural, economic and administrative center of the country. The inhabitants of Ouagadougou are the Ouagalais. The original word is "Woogrtenga" and "Wogodogo" and means "where we receive honors, respect. Originally, the city was called Kombemtinga, the "land of princes". The versions explaining this change of name are numerous and sometimes opposite. More definitely, it can be said that the city was founded in the eleventh century by the Nyonyones. Later, according to the version of Larle Naba, holder of the secrets of the Mossi Kingdom, the founders, undergoing repeated attacks by a neighboring people, had to seek the protection of the Emperor Mossi Zoungrana, then established in Tenkodogo. Zoungrana entrusted the defense of the city to his son Oubri. The Nyonyones surrendered, and the locality was then called "Wogdgo": "Come honor me". It is this name that would have evolved to give Woghodogo, then Ouagadougou in its westernized version. The city became capital of the Mossi Empire in 1441 during the reign of Naba Niandéfo. But it is only in 1681 that it will become permanent residence of the emperors (morho-naba), with Naba Sanem. Neighborhoods are then created quickly around the imperial palace. They have kept their name until now: Ouidi, district of the chief of the cavalry. Dapoya (of), district of freed captives. Paspanga (of), where the newly enthroned emperors will collect the submission of the dignitaries of the Empire, etc. Ouagadougou in 1930. Photograph by Walter Mittelholzer. Louis-Gustave Binger enters the evening of Friday, June 15, 1888 and leaves a long description. Sanem receives him by obligation3 but does not take long to show his desire that he go away. It also prevents him from continuing the planned journey. Binger then decides to return to Boukary Koutou and leaves the city on July 10. Having become capital of the territory of Upper Volta in 1919, the city loses its pre-eminence during the years of division of the territory (1932-1947). At the reconstitution of Upper Volta, the administration is again transferred to Ouagadougou. From the arrival of the railway in 1954 until independence (1960), the population of the city will double from 30,000 to more than 60,000 inhabitants. Subsequently, the number of Ouagalans will double regularly every ten years: 500,000 in the early 90s and more than 1.5 million today.
